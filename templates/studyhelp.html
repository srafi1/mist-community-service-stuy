STUDY HELP

OVERVIEW & PURPOSE

        Studying starts the moment we enter school. But even after all those years of taking tests and quizzes, most of us don't discover the studying tips and tricks that can save us from those all nighters and help us remember information more efficiently until it's too late. If you want to learn some of these tips and studying methods, scroll below!




Note Taking

We've all been there ---we can't find our notes or we can't read our scrawly, messy handwriting. But fear no more, your days of note taking nightmares are over. Here are some tips on how to take proper and effective notes.

Guidelines for Taking Notes
  • Take notes consistently
  • Don’t be concerned with spelling and grammar.
  • Take notes selectively. You should not write down every single word your professor/teach states.
  • Paraphrase what you hear into your own words
  • Jot down major ideas
  • If possible, use pens of different colors and highlight important words.
How to Find Major Points is Lessons/Lectures

Teachers speak so fast that it’s almost impossible to make out what they’re saying, making it hard to understand what they’re supposed to be teaching. If your teacher does one of the following, it most likely means he or she is trying to emphasize an important point.

  • Writes words or phrases on the board
  • Uses repetition
  • Uses key words such as “Now this is important” / “Remember…”
  • Pauses before or after an idea
Ways to Reduce Lengthy Notes
  1. Use symbols to abbreviate:
    • +, & for and, plus
    • = for equal
    • - for minus
    • # for number
    • X for times
    • > greater than, more
    • < less than, smaller
    • w/ for with
    • w/o for without
    • w/in for within
    • / for per
    • ---> for as a result or leads to

      • Example: The diameter of planet x is four times greater than the diameter of y.
      Planet x = 4x > diameter planet y

  2. Abbreviate long words
  3. Use any one of the five methods mentioned below:
    • The Sentence Method
    • The Cornell Method
    • The Outline Method
    • The Mapping Method
    • The Charting Method

The Sentence Method

  • Method: Write every new idea on a separate line, numbering as you progress.
  • When to use: Use this method when the lesson is kind of organized, but it is full of important content. The different points are unrelated.
  • Example: Melville did not try to represent life as it really was. The language of Ahab, Starbuck, and Ishmael, for instance, was not that of real life. → Mel didn’t repr. Life as was; e.g. lang. Of Ahab, etc. no of real life.

The Cornell Method

Systematic method for condensing and organizing notes. This system divided the page up into two sections; the main space and the small, left space. In the main space, record any notes and on the smaller section, label each idea and detail with a keyword or “cue”.

The Outline Method

Dash or indented outlining is best used for most classes, except for classes such as physics or math.
  1. The most general information begins at the left margin. As the information gets more specific, you go towards the right margin.
  2. Indenting indicated relationship between different points.
  3. Though they aren’t necessary, you can organize further with numbers, letters or Roman numerals.

The Mapping Method

Used to connects each fact or idea to one central idea. It is a graphic representation of the lesson. Use when the lecture content is heavy and well organized

The Mapping Method

Used in history or foreign language classes. If the format of the lecture is chronological or mostly definition based, set up your paper by drawing columns and labeling appropriate headings in the table.

Reading

Learning how to read quickly and effectively is a skill most students should develop early on since that is how we get most of our information. Although it can look intimidating at first, implementing these strategies can help save you from reading your textbook/notes a billion times.

  1. Scan the chapter and the main headings
  2. Skim through the subheadings to get a general overview.
  3. Read the bolded words
  4. Read the chapter once, focusing on big ideas
  5. Read one more time to understand the specific points.

Use the following strategies when reading:

  • Read out loud
  • Jot down quick notes as you read
  • Make small doodled or pictures that can help you remember the ideas
  • Outline the chapter as you read
  • Try explaining what you read to someone else

Where to Study

Finding a quiet place where you can focus and concentrate on your studies is just as important as taking good notes or reading. Make sure the location where you are studying is:

  • Distraction free. If distractions do remain, try to eliminate them as much as possible.
  • Make sure you are comfortable and there is good lighting
  • Everything you need should be within reach. You should not have to walk to another room or place to get a book or a pencil.
Possible locations:
  • Your local or school library
  • Barnes & Nobles or other book stores
  • Cafes and Coffee Stores

When choosing a location to study, understand your personal requirements. If you spend majority of your day in school, find a place at your school where you can study quietly without any distractions.

Ask these questions:

  • Do you have prefer to study in a quiet setting or do you prefer having others around you so you can interact with them?
  • What extracurriculars do you participate in? How does participating in these activities affect where you study?
  • Are you mainly studying in one place or does your study place shift depending on the situation?

When Should I Study?

  • You should review your notes for 15 minutes every day or even briefly glance through them for a minute or two. Contrary to popular belief, having intense study sessions leading up to the exams helps you remember a lot more for a specific exam. This is good for short term studying because it's fresh on your mind.
  • A few weeks (or days depending on the type of test) before your exam, make sure to eliminate your distractions. Don't watch tv, avoid social media and don't see your friends as much.

Study Sessions

  • Break up your study sessions into increments of 40 to 90 minutes, giving yourself a five to ten minute break in between.
  • Do not make your breaks to long. A 40 minute session followed by 30 minutes of break is counterproductive.

Scheduling

  • If your school does not provide you with a planner, invest in one. Write down any important dates such as test days, days where you are busy for an extracurricular or days when you’ll relax.
  • Set strict times for studying. It’s usually hard to follow through these times but setting up alarms on your phones or downloading apps such as facebook lock or app lock will prevent you from procrastinating.
  • Set to-do lists. Be specific. Instead of writing “study for chemistry”, write down which chapters and pages.

Study Hacks

  • Use mnemonic devices to memorize and make acronyms.
  • Make flashcards (writing flashcards out is the best way but online websites or apps such as Quizlet or Study Blue help as well.
  • Associate pictures or stories with the key concepts
  • Block distractions (ie apps like Self Control and FacebookLock monitors the amount of time you spend on a website and blocks it based on that).
  • Make study guides / cheat sheets where you describe possible situations you might encounter during an exam and what approach you’ll take\
  • Use practice tests (Problem Attic is a website that compiles regents questions for specific topics from specific subjects from 1998 to 2016).
  • Treat yourself.

Useful Websites and Apps

  • Quizlet: users can create “sets” of flashcards for any subject. The website generates the flashcards for you and you can create practice tests and matching games such as scatter and space run.
  • Go Conqr: this website is specifically oriented for visual learners. Users can create mind maps which breaks down broad topics into smaller sections.
  • Memorize.com: this website is perfect for learning terms and definitions. Users can post articles, images and diagrams to share information on any number of topics.
  • Schooltraq: this is an online digital planner that helps keep due dates in check. It ensures that you never lose your schedule as it syncs between your phone and computer.
  • Khan Academy: this website has tons of video lessons and tutorials for various subjects. The videos are well-paced and in-depth, so you'll be sure to learn what you need for class.
